TWELVE - OPTI-STRETCH
SCIENTIFIC DISCOVERIES

■ ■ ■
  1. Anatomic cause of Benign (Common) Back Pain.
    1. For over 300 years, it has been documented that a common form of low back pain is located over the sacroiliac joint. Many hundreds of researchers have attempted to identify the anatomical cause of this pain.
    2. Muscle complexes responsible for the majority of Benign Back Pain.
      1. Psoas Muscle Complex – for the lower back, sacroiliac area and hips.
      2. Rhomboids – for the upper back [requires correction of (i)].
      3. For the neck, the Trapezius has long been recognized. The new discovery is that correction of (i) and (ii) greatly facilitates rehabilitation.
  2. Anatomical variability and physiologic complexity of the Psoas Muscle Complex.
    1. Several researchers have previously documented there are 3 different forms of the psoas muscle complex anatomy.
    2. Opti-stretch has discovered 2 other different forms of iliopsoas tendon length.
    3. This makes 18 different variants of the psoas muscle complex anatomy found in the general population.
      1. This correlates well with population statistics for back pain.
      2. Helps explain the complexity of Benign Back Pain.
    4. Unless other accessory core muscles are first rehabilitated, the psoas muscle complex cannot be fully rehabilitated.
  3. “Recurrent Back Strain Syndromes”.
    1. “Psoas Short-Leg Low Back Strain”.
      1. Functional leg shortening is nearly always seen.
      2. Anatomic leg shortening is usually not a significant factor.
    2. “Rhomboid Upper Back Strain”.
    3. “Trapezius Neck Strain”.
  4. “ACIM” (Asymptomatic Chronically Injured Muscle).
    1. The major cause of “recurrent back strain syndromes”.
    2. Found only in certain core muscles.
    3. Can develop following acute injury if 100% healing does not occur.
    4. Accounts for the majority of asymmetry seen in body movement.
    5. Other causes of asymmetry include:
      1. Indirect muscle spasm due to Pathologic (Malignant) Back Pain.
      2. Tendon and ligament shortening.
  5. Determination of a healthy back or bad back (trick back).
    1. First need a Doctor’s evaluation to check for Pathologic (Malignant) Back Pain.
    2. Potential for self-assessment and evaluation.
  6. Scientific screening test to indicate whether you have a healthy back.
    1. Opti-stretch screening tests (Step 1 of the Opti-stretch Challenge).
      1. Beginning Program
      2. Intermediate Program.
      3. Advanced Program.
    2. Risk factor analysis of “recurrent back strain” (asymmetry greater than 1 inch).
  7. Opti-stretch Therapeutic Technique (Step 2 of the Opti-stretch Challenge).
    1. “Stretch receptor lengthening”.
      1. Incremental movement technique.
      2. Incremental exhalation technique.
      3. Forced exhalation.
    2. 2 to 6 months of work.
    3. Recognition of “secondary stretch receptors” past the age of 35.
  8. Muscle Tone.
    1. Improved scientific definition.
    2. Accurate, yet simple measurement of the range of motion for core muscles.
    3. Objective measurement of the “first sensation of stretch”.
    4. Four transition points for “stretch to pain transition” in healthy muscles.
    5. Aneroid manometer measurement.
    6. Potential for large scale standardization of healthy muscle tone for all of the various body types.
  9. Maintenance (Step 3 of the Opti-stretch Challenge).
    1. Realistic and goal oriented stopping point.
    2. Time-efficient program.
    3. Cost effective.
    4. First aid for a life-time of healthy muscles.
    5. Activity level most precisely defines and best preserves a healthy back.
  10. “Gold standard” for range of motion.
    1. Stretch receptor lengthening beyond this point appears to offer no significant improvement for rehabilitating a bad back.
    2. Not necessary in all body types.
    3. Screening test for genetic hyperflexibility.
  11. Posture.
    1. Recognition of correctable factors (i.e., ACIMs).
    2. Improved awareness to help provide a life-time of optimum activity.
  12. Involuntary (Instinctive) Stretching.
    1. Found in all non-aquatic mammals.
    2. Comparison to stretching exercises.
    3. Help in determining key stretching exercises.
